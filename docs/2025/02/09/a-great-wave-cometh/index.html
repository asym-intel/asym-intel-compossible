As the Dalai Lama says compassion (a kind of kindness) must be our only philosophy and religion.

The Buddhist concept of compassion is a better approach than the simplistic abstractions of the concept of ’love’, because it is practical. It joins the feeling of wonder and joy at being just a tiny part of all of life’s wondrous forms with how we should act using contemplation and reason.

“...Tell me about despair, yours, and I will tell you mine.
Meanwhile the world goes on.
Meanwhile the sun and the clear pebbles of the rain
are moving across the landscapes,
over the prairies and the deep trees,
the mountains and the rivers.
Meanwhile the wild geese, high in the clean blue air,
are heading home again.
Whoever you are, no matter how lonely,

(Mary Oliver, ‘Wild Geese’)

Although the road to compassion may start as a motive emotion, it is important to realise that compassion is not a surfeit of feeling towards others – an excess of feeling that could be exhausted.

We see this serene universal compassion most in the figure of Gautama Buddha. Compassion is wisdom. This wisdom contains within it a reduction of the pain of the ‘feeling’ of aloneness, and a diminution of a false sense of an important, separate ‘self’. This sharing allows us to connect with the Earth and its other life-forms and the flow of the energy of life. It roots us like a tree and helps us to weather the storms.

It seeks to look beyond the surface of relative form to the deeper nature of impermanence and the commonality of all life. It is a state of mind that requires careful work to maintain.

Whether you are religious in the traditional sense or not you should understand the importance of community, common practices and communal comfort in times of great upheaval and change (an Axial Age).

The importance of humility is crucial too, it means being connected to the earth (humus) and to see ourselves as part of it not above it. It carries us all, whether we think it or not. A new humility for humanity would enrich us all.

Pacifism vs non-violence

Many confuse non-violence with pacifism. For example, reasonable self defence is an act of self love (not hatred or anger directed at others) and so can be considered non-violent (e.g., under a Buddhist analysis). This distinction is crucial to understand, to take affirmative action and, most importantly, to avoid becoming the very thing you should be contesting (let’s call this ‘Israel Syndrome’).
