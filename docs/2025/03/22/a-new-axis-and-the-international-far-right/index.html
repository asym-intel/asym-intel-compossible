“The longstanding effort to keep extremist forces out of government in Europe is officially over.”

(Giovanna Coi, Politico.eu9)

The European political landscape has seen a significant rise in hard-right and far-right groups, encompassing national conservatism, right-wing populism, and neo-fascist ideologies. These groups have achieved electoral success and, in some cases, have become integral parts of national governments.

Opposition parties of the far-right in nations like France, Germany, and Spain represent major political forces with substantial voter bases.

Countries with strongly growing far-right support

  1. Germany: The Alternative for Germany10(AfD) has made substantial gains, securing over 20% of the vote in the February 2025 elections (double their previous share).
  2. France: The National Rally(RN)11 (formerly the National Front) has seen a resurgence, securing the most votes in European elections and leading the first round of early parliamentary elections.
  3. Belgium: The far-right Vlaams Belang came close behind the winning conservative Flemish N-VA party.
  4. Austria: The Freedom Party (FPO) won 29% of the vote in September 2024 elections, the strongest showing for the far-right since World War II.
  5. Czech Republic: Has a hard-right party ANO that seems likely to form the next government.12
  6. Sweden: the Sweden Democrats are now the second largest political party in parliament.
  7. Spain: **Vox**13, the third largest party in the Spanish parliament continues to see its popularity grow14.

Countries with hard-right parties in Government:

  1. Italy: Giorgia Meloni’s Brothers of Italy party leads the government.
  2. Hungary: Viktor Orbán’s Fidesz party holds power.
  3. Finland: Has a hard-right party in a coalition government (though the Finns Party is more similar to the Swiss People’s Party15 - see below)
  4. Netherlands: Geert Wilders’ Party for Freedom 16(PVV) has gained significant support, leading to the formation of the most right-wing coalition government in recent Dutch history.

In Slovakia, although the party in power is not usually classified as hard or far right, the Prime Minister (Robert Fico) is allied with Viktor Orbán.
