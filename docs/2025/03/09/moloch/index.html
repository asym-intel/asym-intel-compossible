Moloch for the Minotaur

A fearful people Can do such terrible things

A fearful people

Can do such terrible things

They are so easily tempted

Frightened into

Wagering with Satan

And guaranteed

Damnation

The people of Canaan

(To appease

Great powers

Or avoid defeat)

Sacrificed

Their most innocent

Some say to Yahweh

A dreadful gift

No din of drums

Could ever drown

The sounds

Of children

Shrieking out

In their

Unbearable

Honour

And yet this cult

Can still be found

Even here in

Great Europa

Where learned

Democrats

Perfect

An age-old

Substitution trick

Gifting the

Gods of war

The beloved

Of another tribe

In place of

Our own

In Gaza alone

Twenty thousand

Or more

Little

Loving

Burnt offerings

Screaming

And this poem

Pathetic expiation

Yet still

The malevolent

Kahanist devils

Can not be placated

And the raging

Blood-lust of the

American bull

Will not be sated

And we

(Who did not

Light the fires or

Wield the knife)

Can never be cleansed

Can never get this

Damned blood

Off our hands

The evolving story of Moloch as a form of child sacrifice: traditionally it was considered to be an old Canaanite god but more recently the evidence points to Moloch being the name for the sacrifice itself. The practice spread to Carthage with the Phoenician founders from Tyre (Lebanon) . Moloch was often described as a practice of placing children inside or on a burning hot metal effigy. The effigy has the body of a man and the head of a bull and its arms are held out ready for offerings. However, it is still not clear whether it was an actual child sacrifice (as seems likely) or perhaps a pagan hangover which involved a painful fiery rite of passage.

Wikipedia is a good start to research this practice and has some great sources. This one is particularly good ‘Human sacrifice in Jewish and Christian tradition. Brill. pp. 109–132. ISBN 978-9004150850.

According to tradition, the Minotaur was a part man part bull offspring of the wife of King Minos of Crete (Pasiphaë) and a bull that she was impelled to fall in love with. This was as punishment from Poseidon to Minos for substituting the bull in a sacrificial offering that was made to Poseidon. The Minotaur was held in a labyrinth in Crete. Every nine years the people of Athens had to choose seven men and seven women to be offered as sacrificial victims to the Minotaur in retribution for the killing of Minos’s son Androgeos in Athens.
